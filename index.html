The Regulation of the Central Intellectual Property and International Trade Court for Oil Pollution Cases arising from Vessels B.E. 2568

Analysis from our Senior Partners on the shifting tides of regional law.

Background The maritime transport of oil involves significant risks, including accidents and operational failures that may result in oil spills and serious environmental damage. To address these risks, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) has developed key international legal instruments to prevent and compensate for oil pollution damage. Two principal conventions were adopted under the IMO framework: (i) The International Convention on Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage 1992 (“CLC 1992”), which establishes the strict liability of shipowners for oil pollution damage; and (ii) The International Convention on the Establishment of an International Fund for Compensation for Oil Pollution Damage 1992 (“FUND 1992”), which provides supplementary compensation when the shipowner’s liability is insufficient or unavailable. Thailand is a party to both conventions and has implemented them through domestic legislation, namely the Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage Caused by Ships Act, B.E. 2560 (2017) (the “Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Act”) and the Requirement of Contributions to the International Fund for Compensation for Oil Pollution Damage Caused by Ships Act, B.E. 2560 (2017) (the “Fund Act”). These statutes establish Thailand’s legal framework governing liability and compensation for oil pollution. The framework has been further strengthened by the Regulation of the Central Intellectual Property and International Trade Court on Oil Pollution Cases Arising from Vessels, B.E. 2568 (2025) issued by the Chief Justice of the Central Intellectual Property and International Trade Court and published in the Royal Gazette on 23 September 2025 (effective 24 September 2025). The Regulation sets out procedures on legal proceedings for oil pollution damage claims in Thailand, providing financial security, and enforcement of foreign judgments, enhancing the practical implementation of the international liability regime in Thailand. 1. Legal Proceedings for Oil Pollution Damage Claims in Thailand In bringing a claim for oil pollution damage, the claimant must file a complaint clearly describing the damage and specifying the relief sought. Pursuant to the Regulation, the damage claimed must fall within the definition of “pollution damage” under section 3 of the Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Act, which includes: (1) loss or damage caused outside the ship by contamination resulting from the escape or discharge of oil from the ship, wherever such escape or discharge occurs, including compensation for environmental impairment and loss of profit resulting from such impairment; (2) the costs of preventive measures and loss or damage caused by such measures. Where compensation under the Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Act is unavailable or insufficient, claims may also be brought against the International Fund for Compensation for Oil Pollution Damage (the “Fund”) in accordance with section 11 of Fund Act. In which case, the Fund is required to pay compensation where: (1) no one bears liability under the Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Act; (2) the liable shipowner is financially incapable of providing full compensation, or existing financial security is insufficient to satisfy the claim; or (3) the damage exceeds the shipowner’s liability limits under the Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Act. Additionally, expenses or property voluntarily expended by the shipowner to prevent or mitigate pollution damage are also considered pollution damage for the purposes of compensation. When a claim is made against the Fund, the complaint must expressly state the applicable ground under section 11. In cases where the Fund is not named as a defendant, the Court must inform the parties of their right to notify the Fund of the proceedings and if any party wishing to exercise this right, it must notify the Court before the first hearing. 2. Security Under the Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Act, a shipowner may limit liability for an oil pollution incident upon providing financial security in the amount prescribed by law. Such financial security may take the form of cash, a bank guarantee, or other forms of security, whether provided individually or in combination, as the Court considers appropriate. Importantly, the security must be readily convertible into cash in order to ensure prompt compensation for affected parties. The Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Act also establishes liability limits based on the gross tonnage of the vessel. For ships not exceeding 5,000 gross tons, liability is capped at 4.51 million Special Drawing Rights (SDR). For ships exceeding 5,000 gross tons, liability is calculated as 4.51 million SDR plus 631 SDR for each additional gross ton above 5,000, subject to a maximum total liability of 89.77 million SDR. Once a judgment in an oil pollution case has become final and a claimant is entitled to compensation, the claimant may file a petition with the Court requesting for payment from the financial security provided by the shipowner. Upon receipt of the petition, the Court must notify the shipowner, insurer, or security provider and order public notice to be posted or published in a manner it deems appropriate. A petition for payment from the security must be filed within 10-year period from the date of judgment. Where the Court grants permission to enforce payment against the security, the available security is applied first to satisfy the claims of those who have filed their petitions earlier, and in the event where the security is insufficient to satisfy all claims in full, the available remaining amount shall be distributed among claimants in proportion to their respective entitlements. 3. Enforcement of Foreign Judgments In Thailand, the enforcement of foreign judgments relating to oil pollution damage is governed by the Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Act and Fund Act. A final judgment concerning oil pollution damage rendered by a court of a State Party to the relevant Convention may be enforced in Thailand. Nevertheless, such enforcement is not automatic. Thai courts shall refuse enforcement where any of the following grounds is established:  Fraud: where the foreign judgment was obtained by fraud;  Denial of Due Process: where the defendant was not duly served a summons and was not given a reasonable opportunity to present his case in defence; or  Public Order and Good Morals: where enforcement of the judgment would be contrary to the public order or good morals of the people of Thailand. With respect to procedural requirements, the Regulations provides that a foreign judgment creditor may submit a petition to the Thai court seeking an order for enforcement, provided that all of the following conditions are satisfied:  the foreign judgment is final and enforceable in the jurisdiction in which it was rendered;  the creditor has not yet received full satisfaction of the judgment debt from the foreign judgment debtor; and  the application is filed within the period during which enforcement remains possible in the country of origin, and in any event no later than three years from the date on which the judgment became final. Jurisdiction over petitions for the enforcement of foreign judgments in oil pollution damage cases lies with the Intellectual Property and International Trade Court, a specialized court with expertise in international and cross- border disputes. Upon the filing of the petition, the foreign judgment creditor is required to serve a copy of the petition together with all supporting documents on the foreign judgment debtor. Following proper service, the debtor is entitled to submit a written objection to the court within 15 days. The Court will then examine the petition and any objections raised. If no statutory ground for refusal is established, the Court may grant an enforcement order. Once such order is issued, the foreign judgment shall have the same effect as a judgment rendered by a Thai court for enforcement purposes. Conclusion Thailand’s oil pollution liability regime demonstrates a comprehensive integration of international standards into domestic law. By combining strict liability of shipowners, access to supplementary compensation through the Fund, mandatory financial security, and the enforcement of foreign judgments, the framework seeks to ensure that oil pollution damage is met with effective and timely remedies. Its strength lies not only in the allocation of liability, but in the procedural mechanisms that translate legal principles into practical compensation. Complementing these substantive elements, Thailand has taken an important step forward by introducing the Regulation as a specialized procedural framework for cases decided under the CLC and FUND regimes. This framework is intended to be used specifically for judgments based on these conventions, thereby strengthening the practical implementation of Thailand’s international obligations. Although the Regulation does not apply to commercial and civil judgments irrelevant to CLC and the FUND, implementation of the Regulation is an essential development of Thai law represents a progressive development in aligning domestic judicial practice with the operational demands of the international oil pollution compensation system. its significance lies in enhancing the courts’ capacity to render decisions grounded in the conventions within a clearer and more structured procedural setting.
